01 — The question

When a tract appears to "drop" 33 points

It is common to see a tract that hovered near 40% child poverty for years suddenly read 10% — and to assume the data is broken. Census Tract 6054.03 (the Wilde Lake community in Columbia) is a textbook case. How can a five-year estimate move that much in a single year?

It usually isn't an error — and it usually isn't really a 33-point drop. It's the margin of error doing exactly what margins of error do in small places. Every tract-level poverty figure published for Maryland is an estimate built from a survey sample, and at the tract level those samples are tiny. The single number on the map is just the middle of a range — and for tracts like this one, that range is enormous. Here is what the Census Bureau actually reported.

Census Tract 6054.03 — Wilde Lake, Howard County

Estimate — the published rate Margin of error — true rate is 90% likely to fall in this band 30% line — ENOUGH eligibility threshold

Notice that the 2022 range (roughly 19%–68%) and the 2023 range (0%–23%) almost touch. Statistically, the "drop" is barely distinguishable from no change at all — both years are consistent with the true rate sitting somewhere around 20%. The point estimate jumped because the survey happened to catch different households; the underlying neighborhood did not transform overnight.

About the green line. Throughout this page, the 30% line marks Maryland's ENOUGH eligibility threshold — a tract must reach roughly 30% child poverty to qualify. That's why margins of error matter so much here: when a tract's range straddles 30% (as Wilde Lake's 2022 range did), a single noisy estimate can flip it on or off the map even though nothing real changed.

05 — How the number is made

How Maryland measures tract child poverty

  1. The source is the Census Bureau, not the state. Every figure comes from the American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year Estimates. Maryland does not calculate or adjust these rates — we publish the Bureau's number for every tract, consistently, statewide.
  2. "5-year" means pooled, not current. The 2024 estimate blends survey responses collected over 2020–2024. Pooling five years is the only way to get a sample large enough to report at the tract level — but it means the figure is a rolling average, not a snapshot of this year.
  3. Small places, small samples. A single tract may have only a few dozen sampled households with children. Each year the five-year window drops its oldest year and adds a new one, so which households land in the sample shifts — and the estimate shifts with it.
  4. The margin of error is part of the measurement. The Bureau reports every estimate with a 90% margin of error. A rate of "43.2%" with a ±24.4 margin really means: we are 90% confident the true rate is between 19% and 68%. Ignoring the margin is how a sampling wobble gets mistaken for a real change.
  5. Compare ranges, not points. When two years' ranges overlap, the apparent change between their point estimates is not statistically meaningful. This tool shows the range for exactly that reason.

So when a tract's number swings, is one of the years wrong?

Usually neither. Each year's figure is the Census Bureau's official estimate for that five-year window. When both years carry wide margins of error and their ranges overlap, the honest reading is that the true child-poverty rate has plausibly stayed in the same broad range the whole time. The headline number moved; the confidence interval tells you how much weight to put on that move. (For the Wilde Lake tract above, the 43% and the 10% are both official — and their ranges overlap.)

Why not just use the highest year, or smooth it ourselves?

Because the moment the state starts hand-picking which year or adjusting the Bureau's figures, the numbers stop being comparable across the other 1,400 tracts. Applying one published source uniformly is what keeps the map defensible. Where the margin of error matters for a program decision, the right move is to look at the range and the multi-year trend together — which is what this page is built to make easy.
